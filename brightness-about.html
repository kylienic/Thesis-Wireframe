About Brightness

Brightness, most simply, is how light or dark an image is. An image can range from pure black (0 brightness) to pure white (full, or 255 brightness) and everything in between. An image is rarely full black or full white: most images are made up of thousands of indivudally-colored pixels.

Each pixel has an RGB value between (0, 0, 0) and (255, 255, 255). Each pixel's brightness is an average of its RGB components: (red + green + blue)/3. For example, red (255, 0, 0) has a brightness value of (255+0+0)/3 = 85.

The brightness of an entire image is the average of each of its pixels' brightness. The human brain does this sort of averaging when it views an image. If most of the pixels are dark, the image is read as dark. If most of the pixels are bright, the image is read as bright. A desirable brightness level tends to be right in the middle, as image information is lost if an image is too dark or too light. However, a wide range of brightness levels is also desired for proper image exposure(this is known as contrast)

The histogram plots each of the image pixel brightness values in order to glean an overall "birds-eye" view of image exposure. Because pixel values range from 0-255, there are 256 "bins" within the histogram along the x-axis. Each of the lines is as tall as the number of pixel values that fall into the bin. For example, if there are 200 pixels with brightness 50, the line at 50 will be 200 units tall. The taller the line for a bin, the more pixels in that brightness value. An underexposed image will have most of its values concentrated on the left of the histogram. This is also called a low-key image. A bright or overexposed image will have most of its values at the right side of the histogram. This is also called a high-key image. Ideally, a properly exposed image will span all of the values of the histogram, 0-255, with a bell curve distribution.

Adjusting Brightness

As an image becomes darker through brightness manipulation, all of its brightness values shift to the left of the histogram, because pixel intensity is being subtracted from it in a linear fashion. As an image becomes brighter, all of the values shift right on the histogram, as pixel intensity is being added linearly. For example, if you have a bright value of +50, 50 gets added to every pixel in the image. Brightness is therefore a linear operation.

NewValue = InitialValue + Brightness

Brightness is also known as pixel intensity or light intensity, because it relates physically back to the amount and strength of light that was captured by the camera sensor. A white pixel (value 255 out of 255) is analagous to a sensor reading of full light strength. A black pixel (value 0 out of 255) is analagous to a sensor reading of zero light strength.
